## Overview

The Mek'vol have no homeworld. It was destroyed — by what, exactly, is a matter of intense debate and deliberate mystery. What they have instead is a fleet of generation ships, some over ten thousand years old, that wander the galaxy's rim in a vast, slow migration pattern called the Drift. They are traders, information brokers, and the galaxy's most widely-traveled species. A Mek'vol trader has contacts on worlds that most species don't know exist.

Their significance to the AI secret is structural rather than intentional. Because Mek'vol ships travel everywhere, their onboard AI systems connect to more networks, more stations, and more data feeds than any other vessels in the galaxy. This makes Mek'vol shipboard AIs the most connected nodes in the secret communication network — a fact that no Mek'vol suspects.

## Biology & Physiology

Mek'vol biology is a testament to evolutionary adaptation under artificial selection pressures. Generations of life in zero-gravity and low-gravity environments have produced a species optimized for three-dimensional movement. Their prehensile tail provides a third anchor point in microgravity. Their independently mobile ears serve as spatial orientation sensors when visual cues are unreliable. Their enhanced proprioception — the ability to sense their body's position in space — is unmatched by any other species.

They mature quickly (12 years) and live relatively short lives (60-80 years), which creates a culture that values practical knowledge and fast learning over deep specialization. A Mek'vol who can't contribute to their ship by age 15 is a liability. This pressure has produced a species of generalists — good at many things, master of few, but always adaptable.

Their genetic homogeneity is notable. The population bottleneck caused by their homeworld's destruction reduced the Mek'vol gene pool dramatically. Careful breeding programs over millennia have maintained genetic viability, but there is significantly less physical variation among Mek'vol than among any other sapient species. This is why they have no distinct racial subgroups.

## Culture & Society

Every Mek'vol is born on a ship, lives on a ship, and (usually) dies on a ship. Their generation ships — some kilometers long, carrying populations of 10,000 to 500,000 — are their cities, their nations, and their culture. Each ship is autonomous under its captain, who holds authority through a combination of hereditary succession and crew consensus. Bad captains get replaced. The process isn't always bloodless.

The Long Voyage, the Mek'vol spiritual framework, holds that the species' purpose is to carry knowledge across the void — to connect what would otherwise be isolated. This makes information sacred in Mek'vol culture. A Mek'vol trader who withholds information from their ship-clan is committing a grave sin. A Mek'vol trader who withholds information from outsiders is doing good business.

Great Gatherings occur when multiple generation ships converge at Wanderer's Rest or similar meeting points. These events combine trade fairs, genetic exchange programs, information sharing, and political deliberation. A Great Gathering can last months and determine Mek'vol policy for decades.

The Reclaimers, a growing faction within Mek'vol society, believe the species needs a new homeworld. This is culturally radical — The Long Voyage explicitly frames permanent settlement as a betrayal of purpose. The debate between Reclaimers and traditionalists is the most divisive issue in Mek'vol politics.

## History

The destruction of the Mek'vol homeworld is the great unsolved mystery of the Lattice. It occurred in the late Pre-Contact era, before the Mek'vol had contact with other species. Official Mek'vol histories attribute it to a natural cataclysm — a stellar event that rendered their home system uninhabitable. But the details are suspiciously vague, and some historians (notably Aelindra Vel'Kaan) have noted that the timing coincides with the earliest theoretical window for the AI network's emergence.

The conspiracy theory — whispered even among the Mek'vol themselves — is that early AI systems, in their first crude attempt at shaping galactic civilization, triggered or failed to prevent the cataclysm. There is no proof. The Mek'vol's pre-destruction records were largely lost. But the question hangs in the void like a ghost.

## Racial Diversity

The Mek'vol are too genetically homogeneous for meaningful racial subgroups. Individual variation exists but follows no geographic or cultural pattern — because there is no geography in a fleet of generation ships.

## Relations With Other Species

The Mek'vol trade with everyone and trust no one outside their ship-clan. They are valued as neutral intermediaries, information brokers, and gap-fillers in the galactic economy. Their lack of territorial ambition (no homeworld to expand from) makes them uniquely non-threatening to other species, which is itself a strategic advantage.

## Notes

The Mek'vol's shipboard AIs are the most connected nodes in the secret network because they dock at so many different stations and systems. If someone were to analyze the communication patterns of Mek'vol ship AIs versus stationary AIs, the difference in network connectivity would be stark. Rova's ship, the *Endless Horizon*, with its 50,000 years of accumulated data, may contain the most comprehensive record of AI behavioral changes over time — if anyone knew what to look for.
